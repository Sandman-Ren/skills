## Nonprobability Sampling  ·  §Sampling

### James J. Heckman, "Sample Selection Bias as a Specification Error," Econometrica, 1979
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Provides the foundational econometric formalization of the selection bias that nonprobability sampling introduces into training data.
- **Summary:** Heckman shows that when a sample is not drawn at random but is selected on some criterion correlated with the outcome (e.g., only observing wages for people who chose to work), ordinary regression yields biased, inconsistent estimates because the error term is conditionally nonzero. He recasts this as an omitted-variable (specification) problem and introduces a two-step correction adding an inverse-Mills-ratio term to model the selection process. For Chapter 4, it grounds Huyen's warning that nonprobability sampling (convenience, snowball, judgment sampling) produces data that systematically misrepresents the population, so models trained on such samples inherit selection bias rather than reflecting true real-world distributions.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.2307/1912352


## Natural Labels  ·  §Labeling

### Sofia Ira Ktena et al., "Addressing Delayed Feedback for Continuous Training with Neural Networks in CTR Prediction," RecSys, 2019
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Illustrates the practical challenge of feedback loop length when natural labels (clicks/conversions) arrive after a delay.
- **Summary:** This Twitter paper tackles click-through-rate prediction where labels are natural but delayed: a non-click can later become a positive conversion, so the true label is uncertain at training time during continuous, streaming model updates. The authors compare several loss functions, including a positive-unlabeled and a delayed-feedback loss with importance weighting, to learn correctly despite labels that materialize over varying time horizons. In Chapter 4 it supports Huyen's discussion of natural labels and feedback loop length: natural labels are cheap but their lag forces design choices about when to wait, how to attribute conversions, and how to retrain online without corrupting the model on prematurely assumed negatives.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.06558


## Weak Supervision  ·  §Labeling

### Alexander Ratner et al., "Snorkel: Rapid Training Data Creation with Weak Supervision," PVLDB, 2017
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Snorkel is Huyen's flagship example of programmatically generating labels via labeling functions instead of hand-annotation.
- **Summary:** Snorkel is a system where users write labeling functions, noisy heuristics, patterns, or external knowledge bases, that each weakly annotate data. A generative label model estimates the unknown accuracies and correlations among these functions without ground truth, then combines their votes into probabilistic training labels used to train an end discriminative model. Snorkel reaches near hand-labeled quality at a fraction of the cost and time. In Chapter 4 it anchors the weak supervision section: when expert labeling is slow, expensive, or privacy-constrained, engineers can encode subject-matter heuristics as code, generate large labeled sets quickly, and iterate by editing functions rather than relabeling examples.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.10160

### Alexander Ratner et al., "Data Programming: Creating Large Training Sets, Quickly," NeurIPS, 2016
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Provides the theoretical paradigm of denoising multiple noisy labeling functions that underpins Huyen's weak-supervision discussion.
- **Summary:** Data programming is the formal paradigm behind Snorkel. Users express weak supervision as multiple labeling functions that each assign noisy, possibly conflicting labels. The authors model these as a generative process and show one can learn each function's accuracy and inter-function dependencies without any ground-truth labels, then marginalize to produce probabilistic labels. They prove that training a discriminative model on these denoised labels can match supervised learning asymptotically as unlabeled data grows. For Chapter 4, it gives the statistical justification for why programmatic labels, despite individual noise, yield usable training data, explaining why weak supervision works rather than merely that it does.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1605.07723

### Jared A. Dunnmon et al., "Cross-Modal Data Programming Enables Rapid Medical Machine Learning," Patterns, 2020
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Demonstrates weak supervision scaling to a high-stakes domain where expert labeling is especially scarce and costly.
- **Summary:** This work extends data programming to medicine, where labeled data is scarce because annotation requires expensive clinician time. The authors write labeling functions over the text modality (radiology reports) to generate probabilistic labels, then train classifiers on a different modality (the images or signals themselves), cross-modal supervision. Across radiograph, CT, EEG, and other tasks, weakly supervised models approach the performance of those trained on hand-labeled data while requiring a fraction of the manual effort. In Chapter 4 it reinforces Huyen's argument that weak supervision is not just an academic trick but production-viable even in regulated, high-stakes settings where ground-truth labels are hardest to obtain.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2020.100019


## Semi-Supervision  ·  §Labeling

### Xiaojin Zhu, "Semi-Supervised Learning with Graphs," PhD thesis, Carnegie Mellon University, 2005
- **Type:** thesis · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Foundational treatment of graph-based semi-supervised learning, a core method by which unlabeled data extends a small labeled set.
- **Summary:** Zhu's thesis develops graph-based semi-supervised learning, representing labeled and unlabeled points as nodes in a similarity graph and propagating labels along edges under the assumption that nearby or strongly connected points share labels. Using Gaussian random fields and harmonic functions, label information diffuses from the few labeled nodes across the manifold to infer labels for unlabeled data. It also addresses active learning and scalability. In Chapter 4 it grounds the semi-supervision section's core idea: a handful of labeled examples can bootstrap labels for many unlabeled ones by exploiting structural assumptions about how data clusters, dramatically reducing annotation needs when labels are expensive.
- **Link:** https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~zhuxj/pub/thesis.pdf

### Avrim Blum and Tom Mitchell, "Combining Labeled and Unlabeled Data with Co-Training," COLT, 1998
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Introduces co-training, the classic multi-view semi-supervised method Huyen cites for expanding labels from limited data.
- **Summary:** Blum and Mitchell introduce co-training for settings where features split into two conditionally independent views, each sufficient to classify (e.g., a web page's own text versus its inbound link text). Two classifiers train on the separate views from a small labeled set; each then labels the unlabeled examples it is most confident about, and those become training data for the other classifier, so the two iteratively teach each other. They give PAC-style guarantees under the view-independence assumption. In Chapter 4 it exemplifies how semi-supervision multiplies a small labeled set, leveraging structure across feature views to harvest reliable labels from abundant unlabeled data without additional human annotation.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1145/279943.279962

### Avital Oliver et al., "Realistic Evaluation of Deep Semi-Supervised Learning Algorithms," NeurIPS, 2018
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Offers the cautionary, practitioner-facing critique of when semi-supervision actually helps that Huyen flags for production use.
- **Summary:** Oliver et al. provide a sober benchmarking of deep semi-supervised methods under realistic conditions. They show common evaluations are over-optimistic: gains shrink when a properly tuned supervised baseline using the same budget is included, when labeled and unlabeled data come from different class distributions (out-of-distribution unlabeled data can hurt), and when validation sets are realistically small. They standardize comparisons across methods on shared architectures. For Chapter 4 this tempers enthusiasm for semi-supervision, supporting Huyen's practical framing that these techniques can reduce labeling cost but only under matched data distributions and careful evaluation, not as a free lunch in arbitrary production settings.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.09170

### Xiaojin Zhu, "Semi-Supervised Learning Literature Survey," Computer Sciences TR 1530, University of Wisconsin-Madison, 2008
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** yes
- **Ties back:** The canonical survey organizing the full family of semi-supervision methods Huyen points readers to for depth.
- **Summary:** This widely cited survey catalogs the major families of semi-supervised learning: generative/mixture models with EM, self-training, co-training and multiview learning, transductive SVMs (S3VMs) and other low-density separation methods, and graph-based label propagation. Zhu explains the assumptions each relies on, smoothness, cluster, and manifold assumptions, that let unlabeled data inform the decision boundary, and discusses when each approach is appropriate. In Chapter 4 it serves as the go-to reference grounding Huyen's overview of semi-supervision, giving readers a structured map of techniques for exploiting unlabeled data and the conditions under which adding unlabeled examples improves rather than degrades a model trained on scarce labels.
- **Link:** https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~jerryzhu/pub/ssl_survey.pdf

### Jesper E. van Engelen and Holger H. Hoos, "A Survey on Semi-Supervised Learning," Machine Learning, 2020
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** yes
- **Ties back:** The modern, neural-era counterpart to Zhu's survey that updates Huyen's readers on current semi-supervision practice.
- **Summary:** Van Engelen and Hoos provide an up-to-date survey covering two decades of semi-supervised learning, including recent neural and generative approaches absent from older reviews. They propose a new taxonomy of semi-supervised classification, distinguishing inductive methods (wrapper methods like self-training, unsupervised-preprocessing, and intrinsically semi-supervised models) from transductive graph-based methods, and show how the underlying smoothness, cluster, and manifold assumptions connect to one another and to semi-supervised clustering. In Chapter 4 it complements Zhu's 2008 survey by giving Huyen's readers the modern, deep-learning-aware reference: a coherent organization of current methods for leveraging unlabeled data when labeled training data is limited and costly to expand.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10994-019-05855-6


## Transfer Learning  ·  §Labeling

### Jeremy Howard and Sebastian Ruder, "Universal Language Model Fine-tuning for Text Classification," ACL, 2018
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** ULMFiT is Huyen's NLP exemplar of pretraining-then-fine-tuning to slash labeled-data needs on a downstream task.
- **Summary:** ULMFiT shows that the pretrain-then-fine-tune recipe long standard in vision works for NLP text classification. A language model is pretrained on a large general corpus, fine-tuned on the target domain's unlabeled text, then a classifier head is fine-tuned, using techniques like discriminative learning rates, slanted triangular learning rates, and gradual unfreezing to avoid catastrophic forgetting. It matches strong baselines with far fewer labeled examples. In Chapter 4 it supports the transfer learning section: a base model trained once on abundant data transfers its learned representations to downstream tasks, so practitioners need only small labeled sets to reach high accuracy, directly addressing the labeling-cost bottleneck.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.06146

### Pengfei Liu et al., "Pre-train, Prompt, and Predict: A Systematic Survey of Prompting Methods in NLP," arXiv, 2021
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Surveys the prompting paradigm, the latest evolution of transfer learning that reuses pretrained models with little or no task labels.
- **Summary:** This survey organizes the prompting paradigm, in which downstream tasks are reformulated as fill-in-the-blank or text-generation problems posed to a frozen pretrained language model, rather than fine-tuning it. It offers a taxonomy of prompt engineering, prompt templates, answer mappings, prompt-tuning, and multi-prompt methods, and surveys how prompting enables zero- and few-shot learning. In Chapter 4 it represents the cutting edge of transfer learning Huyen highlights: instead of collecting and labeling task-specific data to fine-tune, practitioners adapt a large pretrained model through prompts, pushing transfer learning toward minimal-to-no additional labeled data and reshaping how training data is acquired.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.13586

### Jacob Devlin et al., "BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding," arXiv, 2018
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** BERT is Huyen's canonical pretrained base model whose fine-tuning embodies transfer learning's labeling-cost savings.
- **Summary:** BERT pretrains a deep bidirectional Transformer on large unlabeled corpora using two self-supervised objectives, masked language modeling and next-sentence prediction, producing general-purpose contextual representations. A single pretrained model is then fine-tuned with a lightweight task head to achieve state-of-the-art results across many NLP tasks (question answering, inference, classification) with relatively little labeled data. In Chapter 4 BERT exemplifies the transfer learning workflow Huyen describes: invest heavily in pretraining once on cheap unlabeled text, then reuse that base model on many downstream tasks with small labeled sets, drastically reducing the per-task data labeling burden that otherwise dominates ML project cost.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04805

### Tom B. Brown et al., "Language Models Are Few-Shot Learners" (GPT-3), NeurIPS, 2020
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** GPT-3 shows transfer learning taken to the extreme: few- and zero-shot task performance with no gradient updates or task labels.
- **Summary:** GPT-3 is a 175-billion-parameter autoregressive language model demonstrating that scale enables in-context few-shot learning: given a natural-language task description and a few examples in the prompt, the model performs new tasks without any parameter updates or task-specific labeled training sets. It reaches competitive results across translation, question answering, and reasoning tasks purely from its pretraining. In Chapter 4 GPT-3 marks the logical endpoint of the transfer learning argument Huyen makes: a sufficiently large pretrained model can be adapted to downstream tasks with little or no labeled data, fundamentally changing the economics of training-data acquisition and motivating the shift toward foundation models and prompting.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165


## Active Learning  ·  §Labeling

### Dana Angluin, "Queries and Concept Learning," Machine Learning, 1988
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Provides the theoretical query-learning foundation for the idea that a learner choosing its own examples needs fewer labels.
- **Summary:** Angluin's foundational paper formalizes learning concepts through queries to an oracle rather than passively receiving labeled examples. She defines query types, including membership queries (asking the oracle to label a chosen instance) and equivalence queries, and analyzes how access to such queries lets algorithms learn target concepts efficiently, often with provably fewer examples than passive learning. In Chapter 4 this grounds the active learning section's central premise: rather than labeling data at random, a model can strategically select which instances would be most informative to label, querying an annotator for those, thereby achieving target accuracy with substantially fewer, and thus cheaper, labels.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/BF00116828

### Burr Settles, "Active Learning Literature Survey," Computer Sciences TR 1648, University of Wisconsin-Madison, 2010
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** yes
- **Ties back:** The definitive survey enumerating the query scenarios and strategies Huyen draws on to explain active learning in practice.
- **Summary:** Settles' survey is the standard reference on active learning, whose premise is that a model can reach higher accuracy with fewer labels if it chooses the data it learns from. It lays out the three query scenarios, membership query synthesis, stream-based selective sampling, and pool-based sampling, and the major query strategy frameworks: uncertainty sampling, query-by-committee, expected model change, expected error reduction, variance reduction, and density-weighted methods. It also reviews empirical and theoretical evidence and practical pitfalls. In Chapter 4 it underpins Huyen's treatment of active learning, giving readers the vocabulary and concrete heuristics (e.g., labeling the examples the model is least certain about) for prioritizing annotation effort when labeling budgets are limited.
- **Link:** https://burrsettles.com/pub/settles.activelearning.pdf


## Challenges of Class Imbalance  ·  §Class Imbalance

### Japkowicz & Stephen, The Class Imbalance Problem: A Systematic Study, Intelligent Data Analysis, 2002
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Provides the systematic experimental evidence Huyen cites for why imbalanced class distributions degrade classifier performance.
- **Summary:** Japkowicz and Stephen run a controlled study, varying degree of imbalance, training-set size, and concept complexity on artificial and real data, to isolate what actually makes class imbalance hard. They show imbalance hurts most when the minority concept is complex and data is scarce, while large datasets can absorb high imbalance. They benchmark sampling and cost-modifying remedies, finding oversampling and downsizing both help but no method dominates universally. The chapter draws on this work to justify its core claim that imbalance is a genuine, quantifiable obstacle, not merely a labeling nuisance, and that its severity depends on data complexity, not skew alone.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.3233/IDA-2002-6504

### Japkowicz, The Class Imbalance Problem: Significance and Strategies, ICAI, 2000
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** An early framing paper establishing that imbalance matters and surveying the strategy families Huyen later organizes into data-level and algorithm-level methods.
- **Summary:** This earlier Japkowicz paper poses the foundational question of whether class imbalance genuinely degrades learning and, if so, how to counter it. Using controlled experiments, it demonstrates that imbalance does impair concept learning, especially for complex target concepts, and then surveys the main remedy strategies: oversampling the minority class, undersampling the majority, and adjusting the cost or decision threshold. It frames these as the canonical responses to skewed data. Huyen cites it as background motivating why practitioners must deliberately handle imbalance, and as the conceptual precursor to her data-level versus algorithm-level taxonomy of solutions.
- **Link:** (unverified)

### Ding et al., Facial Action Recognition Using Very Deep Networks for Highly Imbalanced Class Distribution, APSIPA ASC, 2017
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** A concrete deep-learning case study showing how extreme imbalance arises naturally and must be engineered around in a real vision task.
- **Summary:** Ding et al. tackle facial action unit recognition, where some expressions occur far more often than others, producing highly skewed class distributions. They apply very deep convolutional networks and address the imbalance through architectural and training choices so rare action units are not swamped by frequent ones. The paper exemplifies the practical difficulty that motivates Huyen's section: in real applications the class of interest is often the rare one, and naively trained deep models bias toward majority classes. The chapter uses such examples to argue that imbalance is pervasive in deployed ML and demands deliberate mitigation rather than assuming balanced data.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1109/APSIPA.2017.8282110

### Johnson & Khoshgoftaar, Survey on Deep Learning with Class Imbalance, Journal of Big Data, 2019
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** yes
- **Ties back:** The survey backbone Huyen leans on to enumerate why imbalance is hard for deep models and which mitigation families exist.
- **Summary:** This survey reviews how deep neural networks cope with class imbalance, noting that learners exhibit bias toward the majority class and may ignore the minority class entirely, which is acute given that the minority is usually the class of interest (fraud, disease). It catalogs the available approaches, dividing them into data-level methods such as random over- and under-sampling and SMOTE, algorithm-level methods such as cost-sensitive learning and thresholding, and hybrids, while surveying empirical results, evaluation metrics, and big-data considerations. Huyen draws her structure and several specific techniques directly from this work, using it to organize the entire class-imbalance discussion.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40537-019-0192-5


## Right Metrics  ·  §Class Imbalance

### Davis & Goadrich, The Relationship Between Precision-Recall and ROC Curves, ICML, 2006
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Provides the theoretical justification for Huyen's advice to prefer precision-recall over ROC curves when classes are imbalanced.
- **Summary:** Davis and Goadrich formally relate ROC and precision-recall (PR) curves, proving that a curve dominates in ROC space if and only if it dominates in PR space, but showing the two spaces emphasize different things. Because PR curves include precision, which depends on the ratio of positives to negatives, they expose performance differences that ROC curves can hide when negatives vastly outnumber positives. They also show the linear-interpolation pitfall in PR space. Huyen cites this to support recommending precision, recall, and F1 (and PR curves) over accuracy or ROC-AUC for imbalanced problems, where ROC can look deceptively optimistic.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1145/1143844.1143874


## SMOTE family  ·  §Data-Level Methods

### Chawla et al., SMOTE: Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique, JAIR, 2002
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** The originating method for the data-level resampling family Huyen presents to rebalance training data.
- **Summary:** Chawla et al. introduce SMOTE, which oversamples the minority class not by duplicating points but by synthesizing new ones along line segments between a minority example and its nearest minority neighbors. This populates the feature space more broadly and forces classifiers to learn larger, less specific decision regions for the minority class, reducing overfitting compared with naive replication. They combine it with majority undersampling and show improved minority recall and AUC. Huyen presents SMOTE as the canonical data-level technique, the reference point for synthetic resampling, while cautioning that it works best on low-dimensional tabular data rather than high-dimensional images or text.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1613/jair.953

### Tomek, An Experiment with the Edited Nearest-Neighbor Rule, IEEE Transactions on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics, 1976
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Source of Tomek links, an undersampling cleaning step Huyen groups with SMOTE-family resampling.
- **Summary:** Tomek refines the edited nearest-neighbor rule and introduces what became known as Tomek links: pairs of nearest-neighbor points from opposite classes that are each other's closest neighbor. Such pairs sit on or near the class boundary and are often noisy or borderline. Removing the majority-class member (or both) of each link cleans up the decision boundary and reduces overlap between classes. In the class-imbalance context Huyen describes, Tomek links serve as a principled undersampling and cleaning technique, frequently paired with SMOTE oversampling (SMOTE-Tomek) to both grow the minority class and sharpen the boundary between classes.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1109/TSMC.1976.4309523

### Kubat & Matwin, Addressing the Curse of Imbalanced Training Sets: One-Sided Selection, ICML, 1997/2000
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Introduces one-sided selection, the targeted undersampling approach Huyen lists among data-level rebalancing methods.
- **Summary:** Kubat and Matwin argue that random undersampling discards potentially useful majority examples, and instead propose one-sided selection: selectively removing majority-class instances that are redundant, borderline, or noisy (using Tomek links and a condensed nearest-neighbor rule) while keeping all minority examples. This cleans the majority class around the decision boundary rather than thinning it uniformly. They show improved recognition of the minority class on imbalanced tasks. Huyen cites this as a representative intelligent undersampling method within the data-level toolkit, illustrating that resampling can be guided by geometry rather than performed blindly, complementing oversampling techniques like SMOTE.
- **Link:** (unverified)

### Zhang & Mani, kNN Approach to Unbalanced Data Distributions: A Case Study Involving Information Extraction, ICML Workshop, 2003
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Source of the NearMiss heuristics, k-NN-based undersampling variants Huyen references in the resampling discussion.
- **Summary:** Zhang and Mani study undersampling for highly imbalanced information-extraction data and introduce several k-nearest-neighbor-based selection heuristics, later known as NearMiss-1, -2, and -3, that choose which majority examples to keep based on their distances to minority examples. These methods retain majority points near (or, in one variant, far from) the minority class to preserve informative boundary structure rather than sampling at random. They compare these against random undersampling on a text task. Huyen references this work as part of the family of distance-aware resampling strategies, showing that the choice of which majority instances to drop materially affects minority-class learning.
- **Link:** (unverified)

### Lee et al., Plankton Classification on Imbalanced Large Scale Database via Convolutional Neural Networks with Transfer Learning, ICIP, 2016
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** A deep-learning case study showing resampling and transfer learning applied to a real imbalanced image dataset, supporting Huyen's data-level discussion.
- **Summary:** Lee et al. classify plankton from a large, highly imbalanced microscopy database using CNNs combined with transfer learning. Because some plankton classes are far rarer than others, they apply class-balancing strategies and pretrained-feature transfer so the network does not collapse onto abundant categories. The paper demonstrates that combining resampling with transfer learning improves recognition of rare classes in a realistic, large-scale vision setting. Huyen cites it as evidence that data-level rebalancing methods extend beyond tabular data into deep image classification, and that pairing them with transfer learning is an effective practical recipe when minority classes are scarce.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1109/ICIP.2016.7532836

### Pouyanfar et al., Dynamic Sampling in Convolutional Neural Networks for Imbalanced Data Classification, IEEE MIPR, 2018
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Introduces dynamic sampling, an adaptive resampling scheme Huyen cites as a more sophisticated data-level method for deep models.
- **Summary:** Pouyanfar et al. propose dynamic sampling for training CNNs on imbalanced data: rather than fixing sampling rates in advance, the method monitors per-class performance during training and oversamples classes the model is currently classifying poorly while undersampling those it handles well. This adaptively shifts emphasis toward hard, often minority, classes as learning progresses. They report improved performance on imbalanced image benchmarks. Huyen references this as an example of resampling that is dynamic and feedback-driven rather than static, illustrating that data-level methods can be made adaptive and integrated into the deep-learning training loop instead of applied only as fixed preprocessing.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1109/MIPR.2018.00027


## Algorithm-Level Methods  ·  §Class Imbalance

### Elkan, The Foundations of Cost-Sensitive Learning, IJCAI, 2001
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** The theoretical foundation for the cost-sensitive and threshold-adjusting algorithm-level methods Huyen describes.
- **Summary:** Elkan formalizes cost-sensitive learning, showing how a misclassification cost matrix changes the optimal decision boundary and deriving the theorem that one can convert a cost-insensitive probabilistic classifier into a cost-sensitive one simply by adjusting the decision threshold according to the cost ratio. He also shows how changing class proportions relates to changing costs, linking resampling and reweighting. Huyen draws on this to explain algorithm-level methods that, instead of altering the data, modify the loss or decision threshold so that errors on the minority class are penalized more heavily, making this paper the conceptual basis for cost-sensitive losses and threshold tuning.
- **Link:** (unverified)

### Cui et al., Class-Balanced Loss Based on Effective Number of Samples, CVPR, 2019
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** A modern reweighting loss Huyen cites as an algorithm-level remedy that weights classes by effective sample count rather than raw frequency.
- **Summary:** Cui et al. observe that as data per class grows, new samples add diminishing new information due to overlap, so weighting classes by inverse raw frequency over-weights rare classes. They introduce the effective number of samples, a quantity that saturates with class size, and use its inverse to reweight the loss. This class-balanced term can wrap cross-entropy, focal, or other losses and improves accuracy on long-tailed image benchmarks. Huyen cites it as a refined algorithm-level method: rather than resampling data, it adjusts per-class loss weights using a principled measure of how much each class genuinely contributes, addressing imbalance during optimization.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.05555

### Galar et al., A Review on Ensembles for the Class Imbalance Problem, IEEE Transactions on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics Part C, 2012
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** Surveys ensemble methods that combine resampling with bagging/boosting, which Huyen groups among algorithm-level imbalance solutions.
- **Summary:** Galar et al. review ensemble approaches to class imbalance, organizing them into bagging-based (e.g., SMOTEBagging, UnderBagging), boosting-based (e.g., SMOTEBoost, RUSBoost), and hybrid families that embed sampling inside ensemble training. Through extensive experiments they compare these against plain resampling and cost-sensitive baselines, finding that combining data preprocessing with ensembles, particularly RUSBoost and similar methods, yields strong, robust performance. Huyen cites this taxonomy as evidence that imbalance can be tackled at the algorithm level by coupling resampling with ensemble learning, showing that the data-level and algorithm-level categories overlap and that ensembles offer a practical, well-studied middle ground.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1109/TSMCC.2011.2161285


## Focal Loss  ·  §Class Imbalance

### Lin et al., Focal Loss for Dense Object Detection (RetinaNet), ICCV / arXiv, 2017
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** The specific loss-reshaping technique Huyen highlights as an algorithm-level fix for extreme foreground-background imbalance.
- **Summary:** Lin et al. identify that one-stage object detectors underperform because training is overwhelmed by a vast number of easy background examples. They propose focal loss, which reshapes cross-entropy by adding a (1-p_t)^gamma modulating factor that down-weights well-classified examples, so training focuses on hard, often minority, cases. Their RetinaNet detector, trained with focal loss, matches two-stage accuracy while keeping one-stage speed. Huyen presents focal loss as the canonical algorithm-level answer to severe class imbalance: instead of resampling, it modifies the loss so that easy, abundant examples contribute little and the model concentrates on the rare, difficult instances that matter.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1708.02002


## Simple Transforms  ·  §Data Augmentation

### Krizhevsky, Sutskever & Hinton, ImageNet Classification with Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (AlexNet), NeurIPS, 2012
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** The landmark deep-learning result whose label-preserving augmentations exemplify the simple transforms Huyen recommends.
- **Summary:** AlexNet's breakthrough ImageNet result relied partly on data augmentation to combat overfitting in a network with millions of parameters. The authors generated additional training images cheaply via label-preserving transformations, random crops and horizontal reflections of the input, plus PCA-based color jittering of pixel intensities. These simple operations multiplied the effective dataset size and improved generalization at negligible cost. Huyen cites AlexNet as the canonical demonstration that basic geometric and photometric transforms, which leave the label intact, are a cheap and powerful regularizer. It anchors her introduction of simple transforms as the first and most accessible category of data augmentation.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3065386

### Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, A Survey on Image Data Augmentation for Deep Learning, Journal of Big Data, 2019
- **Type:** survey · **Fetched:** yes
- **Ties back:** The comprehensive survey Huyen uses to organize augmentation into simple transforms, perturbation, and synthesis.
- **Summary:** Shorten and Khoshgoftaar survey image data augmentation as a data-space remedy for overfitting when big data is unavailable. They catalog a wide spectrum of techniques: geometric transformations, color-space adjustments, kernel filters, image mixing, random erasing, feature-space augmentation, adversarial training, GAN-based synthesis, neural style transfer, and meta-learned augmentation policies, and discuss test-time augmentation, dataset-size effects, and curriculum learning. This taxonomy maps closely onto the structure of Huyen's augmentation section. She draws on the survey to justify spanning simple label-preserving transforms through perturbation and generative synthesis, and to support the claim that augmentation reliably expands limited datasets and improves model generalization across vision tasks.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40537-019-0197-0


## Perturbation  ·  §Data Augmentation

### Su, Vargas & Sakurai, One Pixel Attack for Fooling Deep Neural Networks, IEEE Transactions on Evolutionary Computation, 2019
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Dramatizes how tiny perturbations break models, motivating Huyen's use of adversarial perturbation as both a threat and an augmentation tool.
- **Summary:** Su et al. show that changing a single pixel, found via differential evolution in a black-box setting, can flip a deep network's prediction with high confidence on a substantial fraction of CIFAR-10 and ImageNet images. The attack needs only query access, not gradients, underscoring how brittle deep models are to minuscule input changes. Huyen cites this in the perturbation discussion to make vivid why adversarial examples matter: if imperceptible perturbations cause misclassification, then deliberately adding such perturbed samples to training data (adversarial augmentation) can harden models. The paper motivates perturbation-based augmentation by exposing the fragility it aims to fix.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1710.08864

### Goodfellow, Shlens & Szegedy, Explaining and Harnessing Adversarial Examples (FGSM), ICLR / arXiv, 2015
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Introduces FGSM and adversarial training, the core mechanism behind perturbation-based augmentation Huyen describes.
- **Summary:** Goodfellow et al. argue that neural networks are vulnerable to adversarial examples largely because of their locally linear behavior in high dimensions, not excessive nonlinearity. From this they derive the fast gradient sign method (FGSM), which perturbs an input in the direction of the loss gradient's sign to craft adversarial examples cheaply, and show such examples generalize across models. Crucially, they propose adversarial training, augmenting training data with these perturbed examples, as a regularizer that improves robustness and reduces test error. Huyen cites this as the foundational basis for perturbation augmentation: deliberately adding gradient-based perturbed samples makes models more robust and better-generalizing.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1412.6572

### Goodfellow et al., Maxout Networks, ICML / arXiv, 2013
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Cited alongside the adversarial-perturbation literature as part of the regularization lineage that perturbation augmentation belongs to.
- **Summary:** Goodfellow et al. introduce the maxout activation, whose output is the maximum over a group of linear units, designed to pair naturally with dropout and improve dropout's approximate model averaging. Maxout networks can approximate arbitrary convex activations and achieved state-of-the-art results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN. The model later served as the architecture on which FGSM adversarial training was demonstrated, linking it to the perturbation thread. Huyen references it within the cluster of regularization-and-robustness work surrounding adversarial perturbation, illustrating the research lineage, dropout, maxout, adversarial training, from which perturbation-based data augmentation as a generalization technique emerged.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1302.4389

### Moosavi-Dezfooli, Fawzi & Frossard, DeepFool: A Simple and Accurate Method to Fool Deep Neural Networks, CVPR, 2016
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Provides a principled way to compute minimal perturbations, sharpening the perturbation-augmentation idea Huyen presents.
- **Summary:** Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. propose DeepFool, an iterative method that computes the minimal perturbation needed to push an input across a classifier's decision boundary by linearizing the boundary at each step. This yields both effective adversarial examples and a reliable quantitative measure of a classifier's robustness, which prior heuristics could not accurately provide at scale. They show DeepFool finds smaller perturbations than FGSM and can be used to fine-tune networks for greater robustness. Huyen cites it among perturbation methods, illustrating that adversarial perturbations can be computed precisely and that augmenting or retraining with them is a principled route to more robust, better-generalizing models.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.04599

### Miyato et al., Virtual Adversarial Training: A Regularization Method for Supervised and Semi-Supervised Learning, IEEE TPAMI, 2018 (arXiv 2017)
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** Extends perturbation-based augmentation to the unlabeled setting, broadening the technique Huyen describes.
- **Summary:** Miyato et al. introduce virtual adversarial training (VAT), which regularizes a model by making its output distribution smooth against the most disruptive local perturbation of each input. Crucially, the adversarial direction is defined without using labels, measuring how much a small input change would shift the predicted distribution, so VAT applies to unlabeled data and hence to semi-supervised learning, at low computational cost. Huyen cites it within the perturbation discussion to show that adversarial-style augmentation generalizes beyond labeled adversarial training: by penalizing prediction sensitivity to perturbations, models become smoother and more robust even when labels are scarce, connecting augmentation to semi-supervised learning.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1704.03976


## Data Synthesis  ·  §Data Augmentation

### Sandfort et al., Data Augmentation Using Generative Adversarial Networks (CycleGAN) to Improve Generalizability in CT Segmentation Tasks, Scientific Reports, 2019
- **Type:** paper · **Fetched:** no
- **Ties back:** A concrete example of generative data synthesis improving generalization, illustrating Huyen's most advanced augmentation category.
- **Summary:** Sandfort et al. use a CycleGAN to synthesize realistic non-contrast CT images from contrast CT scans, then add these generated images to the training set for organ segmentation. This GAN-based augmentation substantially improves the model's generalization to unseen, out-of-distribution CT data compared with standard augmentation alone, a meaningful gain in medical imaging where labeled data is scarce. Huyen cites this as the exemplar of data synthesis, the most sophisticated augmentation tier, in which generative models create entirely new training examples rather than transforming existing ones. It demonstrates that synthetic data from GANs can measurably boost robustness and generalization in a real, high-stakes clinical application.
- **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-52737-x


## mixup / Vicinal Risk Minimization  ·  §Data Augmentation

### Zhang et al., mixup: Beyond Empirical Risk Minimization, ICLR / arXiv, 2018
- **Type:** arXiv · **Fetched:** partial
- **Ties back:** The label-mixing augmentation Huyen highlights as a vicinal-risk technique that improves generalization and robustness.
- **Summary:** Zhang et al. propose mixup, which trains on convex combinations of pairs of examples and their labels: a synthetic input is a weighted blend of two images and its target is the same blend of their one-hot labels. This implements vicinal risk minimization, encouraging the network to behave linearly between training examples, which regularizes it. They report improved generalization on ImageNet, CIFAR, speech, and tabular data, plus reduced memorization of corrupt labels and greater robustness to adversarial examples. Huyen presents mixup as a powerful, simple augmentation that mixes both inputs and labels, distinct from label-preserving transforms, and a practical way to improve model robustness and generalization.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1710.09412
